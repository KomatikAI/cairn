# 01 · The Arc

The map of a human life, grounded in what we actually know about how minds and bodies change over time.

---

## Before the phases: four things the map must respect

1. **Capability is multidimensional.** There is no single "ability" that rises and falls. Speed, memory, knowledge, judgment, and emotional skill each have their own trajectory. Any honest map tracks several curves at once, not one.
2. **The phases overlap and blur.** The ages given are population centers, not walls. A person crosses gradually, and the boundaries are fuzzy by nature.
3. **The individual outranks the average.** Variation *between* people of the same age is larger than the variation *between* age-groups on average. The map describes the population; it never overrides the individual in front of you (see Axiom 2).
4. **The map is not the territory.** This model is a tool for designing support, not a license to sort people. Used to *help*, it's a foundation. Used to *rank*, it's a weapon. Axioms 2, 5, and 6 keep it pointed the right way.

## The science spine

Two frameworks and two findings carry the whole model:

- **Fluid vs. crystallized intelligence** (Cattell–Horn). *Fluid* = raw processing speed, working memory, novel problem-solving; it peaks early and declines slowly. *Crystallized* = accumulated knowledge, vocabulary, expertise; it rises and holds into late life. A life is the changing *ratio* of these two.
- **Faculties peak at different ages** (e.g., Hartshorne & Germine, 2015): processing speed in the late teens, short-term memory in the mid-20s, emotional/social reasoning in the 40s–50s, vocabulary and crystallized knowledge in the 60s–70s. **There is no single peak age for "a human."**
- **The Grandmother Hypothesis** (Hawkes and others): humans are among the very few species with a long *post-reproductive* lifespan. Evolution selected for it because elders' knowledge and care measurably raised group and grandchild survival. The third phase is an **evolved feature whose function is the continuation of others.**
- **Erikson's psychosocial stages**: each life stage has a defining task. Two anchor the back half — **Generativity** (midlife: investing in the next generation) and **Integrity** (late life: making meaning of the life lived).

## Phase I — FORMATION · *Absorb*
**Span:** birth → ~25

### The mind
- Explosive synaptic growth followed by pruning; heavy myelination; **peak neuroplasticity** — the fastest learning of the entire lifespan.
- The **prefrontal cortex** — judgment, impulse control, long-horizon planning — does not fully mature until roughly **age 25**.
- Fluid intelligence climbing steeply toward its peak.

### The behavior
- Exploration and risk-taking (adolescence pairs high reward-sensitivity with an immature brake).
- Identity formation, intense social learning, skill and language acquisition.

### What this phase needs
Protection, education, stable attachment, healthcare, play, and — crucially — **a low-stakes environment to make mistakes safely** while the judgment circuitry finishes building.

### What this phase offers
Adaptability, fresh perception, and the entire future of the society.

### How society already encodes it
We *already* gate entry to high-stakes activity by readiness — minimum ages for driving, voting, drinking, office. The principle "absorb before you act" is not new; this framework simply completes it symmetrically at the other end.

---

## Phase II — CONTRIBUTION · *Act*
**Span:** ~25 → ~65

### The mind
- Fluid intelligence near its peak early in the phase, then a slow, gentle decline.
- **Crystallized intelligence rising throughout** — experience compounding into expertise.
- The *integration* of speed and knowledge sits across this broad middle; specific faculties (e.g., social/emotional judgment in the 40s–50s) peak within it.

### The behavior
- Production, building, leadership, sustained responsibility for others.
- **Generativity** (Erikson): the defining drive of this phase is to invest in, mentor, and provide for the next generation.

### What this phase needs
Opportunity, autonomy, meaningful work, resources, time, and support for raising the next generation (parental leave, childcare, healthcare).

### What this phase offers
The load-bearing capacity of the whole society — the engine that supports both the phase behind it and the phase ahead.

---

## Phase III — TRANSMISSION · *Archive*
**Span:** ~65 → end of life

### The mind
- Fluid speed declines — the slowing and tiredness are real and should be honored, not denied.
- **But** crystallized knowledge, vocabulary, and semantic memory remain strong into the 70s; **wisdom, emotional regulation, and perspective-taking often peak here.**
- **Pathological decline (dementia) is a rising *risk*, not the *default*.** Even past 85, the majority of people do not have dementia. This distinction is the ethical hinge of the entire framework: **you support the minority who decline; you do not write off the phase.**

### The behavior
- Mentorship, stewardship, meaning-making, caregiving (the grandparent role).
- **Integrity** (Erikson): making sense of the life lived and handing it forward.

### What this phase needs
Support for genuinely declining capacities, dignity, *purpose and role*, healthcare, mobility when driving ends, and protection from isolation (loneliness is a major and underrated harm in this phase).

### What this phase offers
Wisdom, judgment, institutional and historical memory — **the archive.** And, per the grandmother hypothesis, the very function evolution built this phase for: **the continuation of others.**

---

## The close of the arc — DIGNIFIED DEATH · *Release*
**Span:** the terminal stage of any phase — most often the end of Transmission, but reachable at any age

### Why it is named as its own stage
The three phases above are **capability** phases: they describe what a person can do as fluid and crystallized abilities rise, integrate, and decline. Dying is not a fourth capability phase — there is no faculty that "peaks" here. But it is a **first-class design stage**, because it is a universal, predictable stretch of every human life where the society either keeps its guarantees or quietly drops them. A map that stops at Transmission implies that support ends when capability does. It does not. The arc's close is in scope.

### What this stage is
The stretch in which cure is no longer the honest frame and the work shifts from extending function to protecting the person: comfort, presence, voice, and meaning. It is continuous with Transmission, not a separate population — most people reach it from elderhood — but it is reachable at any age (terminal illness, catastrophic injury), which is why it is named by its task rather than by an age band.

### What this stage needs
Palliative and hospice care, pain and symptom relief, advance-care planning and supported decision-making, family presence and cultural/spiritual practice, caregiver and bereavement support — and a hard floor against abandonment-by-futility: a decision to stop a burdensome treatment must be paired with active comfort and care, never with withdrawal (Axioms 6 and 9).

### What this stage offers
The completion of a life with its dignity intact, and a model of non-abandonment that the rest of the arc is measured against: a society that holds people *here*, where there is no further contribution to extract, is one whose support was never conditional on output.

## The arc in one view

```
capability
   ^
   |             ........ crystallized / wisdom .........
   |        ....                                    ....
   |     ..                                             ..
   |   ./  fluid speed                                    
   |  ./        \....                                      
   | /               \............                         
   |/                             \..............          
   +----|----------------|----------------|----------> age
   0   ~25             ~65              end
   [  ABSORB  ][      ACT       ][   TRANSMIT / ARCHIVE   ]
   learn          build/give         mentor/steward/hand forward
```

*Two curves, not one. Where the fast-fluid line falls, the deep-crystallized line is still high. A society that maps only the fluid curve sees decline; a society that maps both sees a handoff.*

## What this foundation is for

Each phase implies a set of institutions and guarantees — schools and safe-mistake scaffolding for Formation; opportunity and generative support for Contribution; care, mobility, and transmission roles for Transmission; and palliative, hospice, and non-abandonment guarantees for the arc's close, Dignified Death. That mapping is the subject of [`02-society-support-map.md`](02-society-support-map.md). How we measure readiness within and between phases — by capability, not age — is [`03-measurement-and-transitions.md`](03-measurement-and-transitions.md).
