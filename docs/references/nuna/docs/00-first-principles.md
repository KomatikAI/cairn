# 00 · First Principles

These axioms come first because they constrain everything else. Any institution, rule, or transition proposed in this project must pass all of them. If a design serves a phase but violates an axiom, the design is wrong — not the axiom.

---

## Axiom 1 — Support the whole curve

Capability rises, peaks, and declines. A just society supports a person at **every** point on that curve, not only during the high-output middle. The beginning (dependency of the young) and the end (dependency of the old) are not failures to be minimized — they are normal, expected, and built-in stretches of every human life. Design for all three phases as first-class citizens.

**The curve has an end, and the end is in scope.** The whole curve includes its close: dying is part of the life it belongs to, not the moment support is allowed to withdraw. So the arc is designed as **three capability phases — Formation, Contribution, Transmission — plus a terminal stage, Dignified Death**, which is a first-class design target even though it is not a fourth "capability" phase. Where the three phases are about what a person can do, the terminal stage is about what is owed when cure is no longer the honest frame: comfort, presence, voice, and provision. Withdrawing care at the end because "nothing more can be done" is the curve's last and commonest abandonment, and Axioms 6 and 9 forbid it.

## Axiom 2 — Capability is measured, not assumed by age

Age is a **weak proxy** for ability. Faculties peak at different ages, decline at different rates, and vary enormously between individuals. Therefore: where fitness for a task matters, **test the relevant capacity directly.** Age may *trigger* a check (re-assessment more often as risk rises) but age must never be the *verdict*. The instrument is the test, not the birthday.

## Axiom 3 — No restriction without provision

Every limit placed on a person must be paired with a guaranteed alternative. If you take away the right to drive, you owe transit and rides. If you ask someone to step back from a role, you owe them another role and an income. A restriction without provision is abandonment; a restriction with provision is care. **They are never separated in this framework.**

**Corollary — geography (consolidate the asset, never the obligation).** The same rule binds *where* support is delivered, not only *whether* it is. An inefficient facility may be closed and an asset reallocated to higher impact — but only paired with the provision that preserves the served population's guarantee by another mode: telehealth, a staffed clinic, guaranteed medical transport, relocation support. Divest the **building**; the **obligation** is immovable. Allocative efficiency is permitted; abandonment of the few who are left behind is not.

## Axiom 4 — Sacrifice is chosen, never conscripted

Contribution scales with capacity: those with more carry more, by design. But *sacrifice* — giving up something of your own for the whole — is **offered, not extracted.** A person may choose to "stay behind so others continue." That is noble. The moment that same sacrifice is *imposed* on someone who did not choose it, it becomes the cruelty this framework exists to prevent.

## Axiom 5 — The veil-of-ignorance test

A rule is just only if you would accept it **without knowing which side of it you'd land on** — young or old, capable or declining, strong or vulnerable. Before any rule enters the design, ask: *would I sign this if I didn't know where on the arc I'd be standing when it applied to me?* If the answer is no, the rule is out.

## Axiom 6 — Dignity is constant; only capability varies

A person's **worth does not move along the arc.** A newborn, a peak-career adult, and a frail elder have identical standing as human beings. Capability is a variable; dignity is a constant. No support structure may purchase efficiency at the cost of a person's dignity.

## Axiom 7 — The archive is read, not deleted

The final phase is **transmission**, not termination. Elders carry institutional memory, judgment, and the accumulated context of a life — and a healthy society *reads* that archive into the next generation. The phase is participatory: mentors, councils, teachers, storytellers. An elder is the file the next session boots from, not a file marked for deletion.

## Axiom 8 — Design for the long now and the whole

Decisions are weighed against the generations not yet born and the living world we share. A life-course that optimizes only for the person in front of us, today, at the expense of those who come after — or of the one irreplaceable biosphere — is mis-designed. Stewardship is the default time horizon.

## Axiom 9 — When there is no win-win, refuse abandonment, not loss

Some choices have no costless option. A facility must close; a role must end; a finite resource must go to one place and not another. The framework does not pretend otherwise, and it does not flinch from the choice — because **refusing to choose is itself a choice, and usually the cruelest one: drift discards people without even the dignity of a decision.** The desert hospital closes either way; the only question is whether anyone *decided* it, and whether they paired it with provision.

So there is a hard floor beneath every hard choice. We may impose **loss** — a real, named, bounded cost, honestly owned and openly attributed to whoever bears it — but never **abandonment**, the total discard of a person with no provision and no alternative. Loss is the price of a just allocation; abandonment is the failure this framework exists to prevent. When the two are confused under pressure, hold the line at **provision** (Axiom 3), **chosen-not-imposed** (Axiom 4), and **dignity** (Axiom 6): the person may lose something real; they may never lose their standing or their guarantee.

This axiom is also a discipline of honesty. A hard choice is owned, not hidden: name the tradeoff, name who pays, name the provision, and show that it passes the veil test (Axiom 5). A tragic choice made in the open, with the cost on the table, is how this framework breaks gridlock — not by finding a painless answer, but by making the honest one.

---

*If two axioms ever conflict in a specific case, resolve toward the one that better preserves **dignity** (Axiom 6) and **chosen-not-imposed** (Axiom 4). Those two are the load-bearing beams. **Axiom 9** names how those beams hold under the weight of a no-win choice: impose loss if you must; refuse abandonment always.*
